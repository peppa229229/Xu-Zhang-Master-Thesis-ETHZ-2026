Abstract
Somatic mutation-derived neoantigens are important targets for cancer immunotherapy because of their tumor-specific nature. Neoantigen vaccines have been shown to induce potent immunity, particularly in melanoma, which is characterized by a high tumor mutational burden (TMB). Previous studies have also shown that neoantigen vaccination can be followed by epitope spreading, a phenomenon in which vaccine-induced tumor cell death and antigen release broaden the breadth of anti-tumor T cell responses to non-vaccinated epitopes. However, the functional states of neoantigen-specific T cells induced directly by vaccination versus those arising through epitope spreading, and the extent to which they differ, remain unknown. Here, we focused on a patient with advanced melanoma treated with a personalized neoantigen vaccine in combination with anti-PD-1 and anti-CTLA-4 therapy. We established patient-derived antigen-presenting B cell models and combined these with a personalized, robotics-assisted assembled TCR library to perform pooled functional screening for the systematic identification of TCRs reactive to vaccinated and non-vaccinated neoantigens. We validated six unique vaccine-reactive TCRs and identified four candidate TCRs reactive to non-vaccine neoantigens. By integrating longitudinal bulk TCR sequencing of peripheral blood with single-cell transcriptomic data, we found that validated vaccine-reactive clonotypes emerged only after vaccination and persisted in the circulation. In contrast, TCRs recognizing non-vaccine neoantigens, which we hypothesize arose through epitope spreading, were associated with inhibitory or dysfunctional phenotypes within the tumor. Vaccine-antigen-reactive T cells were, by comparison, extremely rare in the tumor, with only a single CD4 memory cell expressing a validated vaccine-neoantigen-reactive TCR, showing a functional but non-proliferative phenotype. Based on the TCR repertoire landscape in this patient, we further hypothesize that the tumor may have limited recognition and infiltration by neoantigen-specific T cells through immune evasion mechanisms such as HLA loss or antigen loss, thereby promoting immune escape and recurrence. In addition, we found that integrating previously established neoantigen-specific TIL signature scores and TCR-pMHC structural modeling may improve the on-target rate of high-throughput screens for neoantigen-reactive TCR discovery.
